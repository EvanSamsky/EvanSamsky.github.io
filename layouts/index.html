Research profile

Studying how institutions shape food security and resilience

I am a graduate student and researcher specializing in food systems, agricultural policy, and Eastern European studies. My work explores how state institutions, food culture, and national identity interact across different political and economic systems.

My graduate research centers on food security and agricultural resilience in Eastern Europe, with particular attention to Ukraine and the wider post-Soviet region. I am currently preparing for thesis fieldwork in Czechia with support from a FLAS Fellowship.

Before graduate school, I completed dual bachelor's degrees in Biology and Middle Eastern Language and Culture at UT Austin. My undergraduate thesis examined the Soviet stolovaya cafeteria system as a form of public food aid and traced its evolution through privatization after 1991.

Current emphasis

Institutional capacity under pressure

Current projects ask how agricultural governance systems respond to conflict, climate risk, and economic transition, and what those responses reveal about state capacity.

Research themes

Three threads that organize the work

01

Food systems and agricultural policy

Examining how production, distribution, and governance structures affect food access, land use, and rural resilience.

02

Post-Soviet transitions and welfare institutions

Tracing what institutional continuity and reform look like after socialism, especially in sectors tied to daily life and public provision.

03

Climate adaptation and political economy

Studying how environmental stress interacts with governance capacity, security pressures, and uneven development.
